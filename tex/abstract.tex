LiDAR sensors provide precise geometric context for autonomous vehicles, but the sheer volume of points emitted by modern spinning and solid-state units overwhelms embedded storage and wireless backhauls. This thesis tackles that bottleneck by treating LiDAR compression as a staged representation-learning problem. Rather than relying on auxiliary cameras or handcrafted codecs, the proposed pipeline stays within the LiDAR modality and progressively increases modeling difficulty so that each stage delivers a verifiable research milestone.

Stage~1 projects each sweep onto a cylindrical range image and trains a residual convolutional autoencoder with quantization-aware latents to establish a transparent baseline. Stage~2 adds temporal residual coding by warping the previously reconstructed frame toward the incoming sweep so that only the innovation needs to be transmitted. Stage~3 hardens the representation against sparse or missing data via masking and generative completion, encouraging the decoder to reconstruct dense geometry from aggressive subsampling. An optional Stage~4 allocates bitrate according to downstream task importance, ensuring that regions containing safety-critical agents are preserved even when bandwidth is scarce.

Collectively, these stages yield a LiDAR-only codec that spans single-frame compression, inter-frame prediction, and learned completion within one cohesive framework. The design emphasizes reproducibility (each stage can be evaluated independently), practical deployment constraints (closed-form bandwidth accounting through entropy coding), and perception alignment (losses applied in both range-image and 3D spaces). The resulting roadmap provides a concrete path for improving reconstruction quality while steadily lowering the communication burden of LiDAR sensing.
